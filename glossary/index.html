Layouts & Sizes

TKL ABBREV

Layout

Tenkeyless. A keyboard layout without the numeric keypad (numpad). Keeps function row and arrow keys. Popular for gaming because it gives more mouse space.

Example: Keychron K8, Drop CTRL — both TKL layouts

75% SIZE

Layout

Compact layout with function row, arrow keys, and navigation cluster (Home, End, etc.) but no numpad. Arrow keys are right next to the main keys.

65% SIZE

Layout

No function row (F1-F12), but keeps arrow keys and a few navigation keys. Very popular portable size.

60% SIZE

Layout

Minimal layout. No function row, no arrow keys, no numpad. Arrow keys are accessed via Fn layer. Most portable and popular with programmers.

Full Size / 100% SIZE

Layout

Standard keyboard with everything: alphanumeric, function row, arrow keys, navigation cluster, and numpad. Largest option.

ANSI STANDARD

Layout

American National Standards Institute keyboard layout. The "normal" layout used in the US. Enter key is wide and horizontal. Left Shift is one key.

ISO STANDARD

Layout

International Standards Organization layout. Used in Europe. Enter key is tall and vertical. Has an extra key (|\) and split left Shift.

HHKB LAYOUT

Layout

Happy Hacking Keyboard. A special 60% layout that moves Control to where Caps Lock usually is, and moves some other keys around. Popular with programmers but takes adjustment.

Southpaw LAYOUT

Layout

A layout with the numpad on the left side instead of the right. For left-handed number entry or for keeping mouse space while having a numpad.

Keycap Profiles

Cherry Profile

Keycap Profile

The "standard" sculpted profile. Rows have different heights for ergonomic typing. Lower than OEM, great for both typing and gaming.

GMK, ePBT, Akko — use Cherry profile

OEM Profile

Keycap Profile

Tall, sculpted profile found on most pre-built keyboards. Slightly higher than Cherry. If you've used a "normal" keyboard, you've used OEM.

SA Profile

Keycap Profile

Super tall sculpted profile. Looks vintage, sounds deeper. Takes adjustment because of the height. Popular for aesthetics.

Signature Plastics makes classic SA sets

DSA / XDA Profile

Keycap Profile

Flat (uniform) profiles — all keys same height. DSA is slightly shorter. Great for swapping key positions since no row is taller than another.

KAT / KAM Profile

Keycap Profile

Sculpted but with large, cylindrical tops. KAT is mid-height, KAM is lower. Distinctive look with rounded edges on every key.

MT3 Profile

Keycap Profile

Tall sculpted profile designed by Matt3o. Super deep sound, very sculpted. Feels like a typewriter. Love it or hate it.

Materials & Manufacturing

PBT MATERIAL

Keycap Material

Polybutylene Terephthalate. Premium plastic for keycaps. Doesn't get shiny from finger oils, textured feel, lasts longer. Preferred over ABS by enthusiasts.

ABS MATERIAL

Keycap Material

Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene. Smooth plastic that "shines" over time where you touch it. Can be made thinner for RGB shine-through. GMK keycaps are ABS (high quality).

Doubleshot

Manufacturing

Two-color injection molding process. The legend (letter) is a different color plastic molded through the keycap. Won't wear off — the letter IS the keycap.

Dye-Sub

Manufacturing

Dye Sublimation. Heat-presses dye into the plastic. Permanent but can't be done on dark keycaps (can't dye black plastic). Used on many PBT sets.

PVD Coating

Manufacturing

Physical Vapor Deposition. Coating process for metal parts (brass, stainless steel). Makes mirror finishes possible and prevents tarnishing.

Anodizing

Manufacturing

Electrochemical process that adds color to aluminum. The dye is in the metal, not on top. More durable than paint. Common for aluminum keyboard cases.

Switch Terms

Actuation Force

Switch Spec

How hard you have to press before the key registers, measured in grams. Light (45g) = easy to press. Heavy (80g+) = prevents accidental presses.

Bottom Out

Switch Spec

Pressing the key all the way down until it can't go further. The "bottom out force" is higher than actuation force.

Pre-Travel / Actuation Point

Switch Spec

How far you press before the switch activates. shorter = faster. Most switches are ~2mm, speed switches can be ~1mm.

Total Travel

Switch Spec

How far the switch moves from top to bottom. Standard is ~4mm. Shorter travel can feel snappier.

Spring Ping

Issue

Metallic "ping" sound from the spring inside the switch when you press it. Can be fixed with switch lubrication or oiling the springs.

Stem Wobble

Issue

How much the switch stem (the + shape that holds the keycap) moves side to side. Some is normal, too much feels sloppy. Can be reduced with films.

Housing

Switch Part

The plastic shell of the switch. Has an upper (top) and lower (bottom) housing. Different materials (nylon, polycarbonate) affect sound.

Stem

Switch Part

The central part of the switch. Has a + shape on top that the keycap grabs. The stem shape determines switch type (MX-style is cross-shaped).

Leaf

Switch Part

The metal contact inside the switch that registers the keypress. When you press down, the stem pushes the leaf to complete a circuit.

MX-Compatible

Standard

The + shape stem that fits Cherry MX-style keycaps. The industry standard. If a switch is MX-compatible, it fits standard keycaps.

Box Switch

Switch Type

Kailh switches with a box around the stem. Waterproof, less stem wobble, different feel. Can crack some GMK keycaps due to tight tolerances.

Tactile Bump

Switch Feature

A physical "bump" you feel when the key actuates. Different switches have different bump shapes. Holy Pandas have a round bump, Cherry Browns a scratchy small one.

Brand Names

GMK

Brand

German Manufacturer of Keycaps. Premium ABS keycaps made in Germany. High quality, limited runs, expensive. The gold standard for keycap sets.

JTK

Brand

Chinese keycap manufacturer. ABS doubleshot, good quality, more affordable than GMK. Often makes colorways similar to popular GMK sets.

ePBT / EnjoyPBT

Brand

Popular PBT keycap manufacturer. Dye-sub quality, Cherry profile, affordable. Great entry point for aftermarket keycaps.

Zealios / Zeal

Brand

Premium switch and parts brand founded by Zeal. Zealios switches (tactile) and Tealios (linear) are high-end enthusiast favorites.

Gateron

Brand

Chinese switch manufacturer. Very popular budget option. Often smoother than Cherry MX. Makes Yellows, Reds, Browns, and premium Ink switches.

Kailh

Brand

Another major Chinese switch maker. Box switches, speed switches (short travel), and Pro line. Also makes low-profile switches.

Cherry MX

Brand

Original mechanical switch company from Germany. MX Reds, Blues, Browns are the "classic" switches. Still popular but many prefer smoother modern alternatives.

Build Terms

Hot-Swap

Feature

PCB with sockets that let you pull switches out and push new ones in. No soldering needed. Essential for beginners and anyone who wants to try different switches.

Gasket Mount

Mounting Style

Sandwich-style mounting where the plate sits on foam gaskets. Makes uniform sound and cushioned feel. Very popular premium feature.

Tray Mount

Mounting Style

Basic mounting where PCB/screws screw into the bottom of the case. Often creates inconsistent feel. Common on budget boards.

Top Mount

Mounting Style

Plate attaches to the top case piece. Firm, consistent typing feel. Used on many mid-range customs.

Plateless / PCB Mount

Build Style

Switches mounted directly to PCB with no plate. Very flexible feel, bouncy. Requires careful switch insertion so pins don't bend.

Flex Cut

PCB Feature

Slots cut into the PCB to let it bend more. Makes typing feel softer and bouncier. Common on modern PCBs.

Mill-Max

Mod

Tiny metal sockets you solder into a normal PCB to make it hot-swap. DIY conversion for soldered boards.

205g0 / Krytox

Lubricant

Premium switch lubricant. 205g0 is the "standard" (thick, buttery). Makes switches smoother and reduces scratchiness. Used for lubing stems and housings.

Dielectric Grease

Lubricant

Thick grease used for stabilizers (not switches). Prevents rattle on spacebar and other big keys.

Stabilizer Mods

Modifications

Ways to improve stabilizers: Clipping (remove feet for smoother downstroke), Lubing (reduce rattle), Band-aid mod (padding where stabs hit PCB).

PE Foam / Tape Mod

Sound Mod

Putting thin foam or painter's tape on the back of the PCB. Changes the sound profile, often makes it "poppier" or more marbly.

Force Break

Sound Mod

Putting tape or other material between case halves where they meet. Prevents case from "pinging" when typing.
