What is a Mechanical Keyboard? The Complete Guide

February 20, 2025 · 15 min read · Keebshelf

A mechanical keyboard is a keyboard where each key has its own physical switch underneath, rather than a rubber dome or membrane. When you press a key, a stem inside the switch physically completes an electrical circuit, sending the signal to your computer.

Compared to the membrane keyboards found on most laptops and cheap peripherals, mechanical keyboards offer:

  • Better tactile feedback: You feel exactly when the key actuates
  • Longer lifespan: 50-100 million keystrokes vs. 5 million for membrane
  • Customization: Swap switches, keycaps, cases, and more
  • Distinct sound: From silent to thunderous

Modern mechanical keyboards range from $50 mass-market boards to $500+ custom builds. This guide breaks down what's worth paying for and how to choose your first board.

How Mechanical Keyboards Work

Inside every mechanical keyboard switch is a simple mechanism:

  1. You press the keycap, which pushes down the stem
  2. The stem compresses a spring
  3. At a specific point (the actuation point), metal contacts connect
  4. This completes a circuit, sending the keypress signal
  5. When you release, the spring pushes the stem back up

This single-switch-per-key design is exactly what makes mechanical keyboards reliable and repairable. If a switch dies, you replace that one switch (about $0.50-2.00), not the entire keyboard.

Key Terms to Know

  • Actuation: The point where the key registers (typically 2mm into a 4mm total travel)
  • Key travel: How far the key moves down (typically 3-4mm)
  • Hot-swap: A socket that lets you change switches without soldering
  • PCB: The circuit board that connects switches to your computer

Switch Types Explained

The switch determines feel and sound. Here's how the three main types differ:

Type Feel Sound Best For Popular Examples
Linear Smooth, no bump Quiet to moderate Gaming, fast typing Cherry MX Red, Gateron Yellow, Kailh Speed Silver
Tactile Bump at actuation Moderate Typing, all-purpose Cherry MX Brown, Gateron Brown, Holy Panda
Clicky Bump + audible click Loud Those who love the noise Cherry MX Blue, Kailh BOX White, Nintendo-switch style

Our Pick: Gateron Yellow (Linear)

For first-time buyers, we recommend Gateron Yellow switches. They're smooth (linear), affordable, and have a perfect weight—heavier than Cherry MX Red (so you don't accidentally press keys) but lighter than Cherry MX Black (so you don't fatigue).

A 70-pack costs around $25, significantly less than Cherry's $50-70.

Keyboard Layouts: Sizes & Shapes

Layout is about what keys you keep and which you sacrifice. Smaller boards free up desk space. Larger boards have dedicated function keys and number pads.

75% 81 Number pad + some nav Compact but functional $100-250
65% 67 Function row Minimal but practical $150-300

Our Pick for Beginners: 75% or TKL

Start with a 75% or TKL layout. You keep arrow keys and most functions, but save desk space. The Keychron Q1 (75%) is a popular entry point at around $180.

Keycaps: The Visible Difference

Keycaps are the first thing you see and the first thing you replace. Three things matter:

1. Material

  • ABS plastic: Cheaper, shines over time, can feel "mushy"
  • PBT plastic: More durable, textured/stiff feel, doesn't shine

2. Profile

How tall and what shape:

  • Cherry profile: Standard, sculpted rows, great for typing
  • OEM profile: Taller but common

3. Legends (The Letters)

  • Dye-sub: Dye embedded into plastic, won't wear off

For a first set, get PBT dye-sub keycaps in Cherry profile. Around $30-50.

Build vs. Buy: Which Should You Do?

Your first mechanical keyboard decision: buy a pre-built, or build your own?

Buy Pre-Built If:

  • You need a working keyboard tomorrow
  • You don't want to research individual parts
  • You want a warranty
  • Budget is $50-150

Build Your Own If:

  • You want a specific switch/sound/feel
  • You enjoy the assembly process
  • You want a unique keyboard nobody else has

Frequently Asked Questions

Are mechanical keyboards actually better for typing?

For most people, yes—but not for everyone. The tactile feedback helps some typists be more accurate, and the customization lets you optimize for your specific hands.

How long do mechanical keyboards last?

With quality switches rated for 50 million+ presses, a mechanical keyboard can last decades. The keycaps may shine or fade after 5-10 years, but they're replaceable for $30-50.

Are mechanical keyboards loud?

They can be, but don't have to be. Linear switches with proper dampening are quieter than most laptop keyboards. Clicky switches (Cherry MX Blue) are genuinely loud—fine at home, obnoxious in an open office.

What's the best mechanical keyboard for gaming?

Gaming keyboards prioritize fast actuation and N-key rollover. Look for linear switches (Cherry MX Red/Speed Silver, Gateron Yellow), 1000Hz polling rate, and TKL or 75% layout for mouse space.

Can I use a mechanical keyboard with my laptop?

Yes. All modern mechanical keyboards connect via USB-C or USB-A. Some support Bluetooth for wireless use. Your laptop will recognize it as a standard keyboard immediately—no drivers needed.

Wireless vs. wired: why would I choose one over the other?

The choice depends on your priorities and setup:

Choose wireless (Bluetooth/2.4GHz) if:

  • You want a clean, cable-free desk aesthetic
  • You switch between multiple devices (laptop → desktop → tablet)
  • You work from a couch or different positions away from your device
  • You travel frequently and want minimal cables

Choose wired if:

  • You want zero input lag for competitive gaming
  • You hate charging and never want to think about batteries
  • You work in an environment with potential wireless interference
  • You want the lowest possible cost (wired boards are cheaper)
  • You need USB passthrough for a mouse or USB devices

The reality: Modern wireless keyboards have negligible latency (under 5ms). Battery life ranges from 2-6 months depending on RGB usage. Many boards offer both modes—wired for gaming, wireless for everything else. Unless you're a CS2 pro with frame-perfect requirements, wireless is probably fine for you.
